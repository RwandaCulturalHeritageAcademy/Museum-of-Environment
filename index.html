Solar system

The solar system is a group of eight planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune) and other celestial bodies that revolve around the sun. A planet is a celestial body that orbits the sun, has sufficient mass to assume nearly a round shape, illuminated by the light from the sun and cleared the neighborhood around its orbit. Dwarf planet is a planet that has not cleared the neighborhood around its orbit. Among other celestial bodies that revolve around the sun include natural satellites (moons) asteroids, comets and meteoroids.

Earth planet

Earth planet is a dense ball about 12,750 km across and it is the third from the Sun (150 million km ). About 71% of its surface is covered by water, the rest by land. The landmass of the Earth is distributed by the following continents: Africa, America, Antarctica, Asia, Europe and Oceania . The planet is orbited by one satellite, the Moon. The Earth’s atmosphere is primarily Nitrogen (N2), 78.08 %; Oxygen (O2), 20.95 %; and Argon, 0.93%; with trace amounts of carbon dioxide and water vapor.

Earth structure

Earth is made of four main layers

  • The crust: is a solid layer of rock on the surface of the planet. Land is made of continental crust of 8 km to 70 thick and made mostly from granite rock. The layer beneath the ocean bed is made of oceanic crust of about 8 km thick and made mainly from basalt rock.
  • Mantle: a semi-liquid layer of magma or molten rock(iron, oxygen, silicon, magnesium and aluminium) that moves due to convection currents.
  • Outer core: The liquid mainly of Iron, Nickel, Sulphur and oxygen. The movement of its metals creates our planet's magnetic field.
  • Inner core: It is mainly made of Iron and Nickel. It is hot enough to melt but is solid because of the pressure pushing down on it. The temperature reaches 5,500 °C.

Tectonic plate's movement

The earth’s crust is broken into separate pieces called tectonic plates. Each tectonic plate is free-floating and can move independently. Earthquakes and volcanoes are the direct result of the movement of tectonic plates at fault lines (the boundary between tectonic plates). The movement of the plates creates three types of tectonic boundaries: convergent, divergent, and transform.

  • Convergent boundary (destructive edge): Plates move into one another.
  • Divergent boundary (constructive edge): Plates move apart.
  • Transcurrent boundary or transform edge: Plates move sideways in relation to each other.

The Congo Nile Ridge, Volcanoes and high mountains found in Rwanda are the results of these tectonic plate movements.

Continental drift

The separation of the earth continents is attributed to the result of tectonic plate movement. Earth's continents are constantly changing, moving and rearranging themselves over millions of years and this change overtime affects climate and life on Earth

Climate and habitat

Seen from space, the great habitats of the Earth show themselves in different colors: the dark blue of the oceans predominates, the eternal ice shines white. On the continents, the deserts shimmer in a light brown, the green band around the equator shows the primeval forests of Africa and the Amazon in South America. The middle latitudes in the northern as well as southern half of the globe appear in brown as well as green colors - so in Europe and northern Asia as well as North America and in southern Africa and the Philippines. From the equator to the two poles of the earth, the climate changes and determines the diversity and design of the habitats of plants, animals and humans. This also applies for the forests and savannahs in the tropics, the deserts in the arid regions, different seasons in the midlatudes and for the oceans with their huge habitats of animals and plants. Due to the Earth‘s orbit around the energy giving sun and its own rotation, the climate changes from the equator to the two poles in the north and south. In the individual climate zones, certain temperature and humidity conditons prevail, which have led to the development of characteristic communities of plants, animals and humans. The different temperatures of the oceans contribute significantly to the climac changes, mainly because they are responsible for many winds that are triggered by different temperatures of the water surfaces.

Oceans and Climate

71% of the Earth‘s surface is covered by salty seawater, less than only 1% by drinkable freshwater, 2% by ice and snow and only 26% is dry land and thus habitable by humans. In the northern hemisphere, the proportions of land and water are almost equal; in the southern hemisphere, the seas predominate. The seas or oceans are the earth‘s air conditioning system. Only the upper two metres of the oceans can store as much heat as the entire atmosphere. Rain is mainly caused by the evaporation of the water above the sea.

How do the oceans influence the climate?

The importance of the oceans as an energy store is related to their vast expanse and depths of up to 4,000 metres, which can absorb, store and release huge amounts of heat. Warming and cooling of water occurs more slowly than on land mountains and forests like fields. The air masses coming from the sea have a significant influence on the climate of the continents: regions near the coast have a balanced climate with mild winters and moderately warm summers. Humidity and rainfall are high almost all year round. Further, inland on the continents, the temperature differences between day and night, between seasons are greater and there is less rainfall.

Ocean currents

As water temperatures decrease from the equator towards the poles, currents occur as a result of the energy balance between cold (lowerenergy) and warm (higher-energy) water. Driven by winds, the surface water of the currents towards the poles becomes colder, denser and heavier, so that it sinks in the polar regions. Very cold currents at depth then flow back towards the equator. The current cycle is closed - the cold water warms up, becomes lighter and rises.

Nutrients of the oceans

Ocean currents not only influence the climate, they also transport nutrients over great distances. The ocean depths are constantly enriched by dead plants and animals. The cold underwater currents transport these nutrients to southern regions, where they gradually rise to upper regions and contribute significantly to the abundance of fish catches. In the tropical seas around the equator, however, stable water tends to occur throughout the year because of constant solar radiation. Although algae growth is stimulated by solar energy, mixing with cold, nutrientrich water hardly takes place, so that this water suffers more quickly from overfishing.

The deserts-arid climate

A climate is described as dry or arid when precipitation cannot permanently compensate for the loss of water through evaporation. Such arid regions are found on all continents of the earth. Deserts, the driest areas on earth, cover one third of the entire continental surface.

How do deserts develop? Subtropical west winds

Most arid areas lie in a strip on either side of the equator - i.e. around the Tropic of Cancer.The trade winds move the air masses that have rained down over the equator both northwards and southwards, warming them increasingly. Over land, they to a hot and very dry climate. under these conditions, plants can hardly or not at all develop and the largest desert regions on earth form, such as the Sahara and the Karahari in Africa or the Australian desert.

Coastal deserts

Along the west coasts in the tropical and subtropical regions of the continents, deserts are formed by the rise of cold water masses that meet hot desire. As a result, cold ground lust is pushed under a warm lust layer, which prevents rain from forming. Well-known coastal deserts are the Atacama Desert in Chile and Peru (South America) and the Namibia in southwestern Africa.

Landlocked and relief deserts

Humid air masses become colder as they rise up steep mountain ranges and rain down. In the rain shadow on the other side of the mountains, the dry and warm downdrafts create so-called relief deserts. Desert areas also develop in the interior of large continents, where hardly any moisture arrives with the air. Examples are the Takla Makan and Gobi deserts in Asia and the deserts in the interior of Australia

Not simply sand!

Deserts do not always consist of sand. Rock deserts occupy much larger areas worldwide than the well-known sand deserts. The Sahara, for example, is 70 % rocky desert, characterised by boulders and gravel. They are the result of the physical weathering of the bedrock by strongly changing temperatures and the effect of the wind. If the rocks become smaller and smaller as a result, scree and gravel deserts are formed. Sand deserts form as a result of progressive weathering.

Hot during the day - cold at night

In both sandy and stony deserts, the surface heats up very quickly - at night, on the other hand, the surface cools down quickly - partly because there are no clouds to prevent it from radiating too quickly. This means that temperature differences of up to 40 degrees can be endured.weathering.

Climate of the middle latitudes

The mid-latitudes are the areas between the Arctic Circle and the Tropic of Cancer in both hemispheres. In the northern hemisphere are countries such as Canada, the USA, Russia and European countries – in the southern hemisphere countries such as Chile, Argentina, southern Africa and Australia.Distinctive seasons such as cold winters and warm summers determine the climate. High and low pressure areas, provide changeable weather throughout the year. Life in the mid-latitudes is decisively shaped by the seasons. Plants and animals have to survive cold winters and often dry summers.
The climatic diversity of this zone has given rise to different habitats: coniferous forests, deciduous and mixed forests, steppes, winter-cold deserts or Mediterranean hardwoods (e.g. olive trees). The cold seas of the mid-latitudes are important as a climatic factor and because of their abundance of fish as a source of food for humans.

What shapes the mid-latitudes?

The seasons

Due to the oblique position of the earth‘s axis in relation to its orbit around the sun, both the northern and southern hemispheres are inclined towards the sun for half of the year. The sun‘s rays hit the earth more steeply and with more energy, it gets warm, the days get longer and the nights shorter. Due to the flattening of the angle of incidence of the rays in the second half of the year, they become lower in energy: it gets cooler and now the nights are longer than the day. The mid-latitudes know four seasons: Spring, summer, autumn and winter.

The West Winds

In the mid-latitudes, warm air from the tropics meets cold air from the poles. The earth‘s rotation deflects air flowing out from the equator to the east - this is how the westerly winds are formed. In the course of the year, these shift both to the north - then warm subtropical air flows e.g. to Europe - it becomes warm. If it shifts to the south it becomes cold and it is winter. In South Africa in thesouthern hemisphere it is the opposite.

The specificity of Europe

Compared to the mid-latitudes of other continents, Europe has some special features: The ocean current flowing north along the west coast (Gulf Stream) carries warm water masses from the subtropics up to the north of Norway, thus warming the entire coast of northern Europe. In the south, the Mediterranean Sea, which is warm all year round, influences the climate of the surrounding countries. The mountain range of the Pyrenees and the Alps, which run across from west to east, protect the north from particularly hot southern winds and the south of Europe from the cold northern winds.

Tropical climate

Geographically, the tropics are the area between the equator and the tropics. Here you will find both hot and humid climates (Congo) and very dry climates. Because of the year-round sunshine, there is no cool season - so the average temperature remains constant throughout the year.

What characterises the tropics

The sun

Throughout the year, the sun is higher than in the middle and higher latitudes and twice a year it is even vertical – at the zenith. As a result, the earth‘s surface warms up faster and more strongly than in the north or south of the globe. Due to the position of the equator in relation to the sun, the length of the day is always 12 hours. In Rwanda, for example, there is only a deviation of about 40 minutes – in Germany of about 4-5 hours. In South Africa in thesouthern hemisphere it is the opposite.

Persistent low pressure and the trade winds

The heat at the equator causes humid air to rise, the air pressure to fall and so a world-wide area of low pressure stretches along the equator. The rising air creates winds that generate a cycle: warm air flowing in from the ground and falling air masses cooling to the north or south: the trade winds. When these winds blow in from the sea, they bring warm, humid air with them, which rains down in the mountains and forests – for example, in Rwanda in the Nyungwe or Birunga Mountains.

The tropical rain

Because the warm, humid air masses cool down very quickly as they rise near the equator, the moisture condenses and clouds form. This is how the typical cloudbursts with large amounts of water occur regularly in the afternoon. The tropical belt along the equator is about 5,000km wide. In the inner and always humid tropics, between 1,000 and 1,500 mm of precipitation fall in a year – twelve times as much as in the middle latitudes (e.g. North America or Europe). In these areas are the large rainforest regions such as the primeval forest in Central Africa (Congo) or in South America at the Amazon (Brazil). The closer one approaches the tropics, the more the aridity increases, the transition to a subtropical dry climate such as the savannahs in Mali, Niger and Chad.

Habitats of the tropics

In the permanently humid tropical climate near the equator, evergreen rainforests extend across Africa, South America and Southeast Asia. With increasing distance from the equator, the frequency and quantity of rainfall decreases and deciduous tree species increase. Evergreen forests (e.g. Nyungwe) are replaced by rainforests during prolonged drought. Where lack of rain or unfavourable soil conditions prevent forest growth, savannahs develop - tropical grasslands with individual trees and shrubs (Akagera National Park and the expanses in Tanzania).

Equatorial rain forest such as the Nyungwe

Permanent moisture and plenty of sunlight enable lush growth and enormous species richness in tropical forests all year round. The diversity of tree species alone is spectacular, with hundreds of species per hectare; in the whole of Central Europe, for example, there are only 40 species. At a height of 30-40 m, a more or less closed canopy forms. Below this, the various shrub and tree species reach different heights depending on the amount of light available. Most tree trunks are straight and slender and have few branches below the crown. The occurrence of flowers and fruits directly on the trunk is widespread, leaves with elongated leaf tips from which the water film drips off are characteristic.
Board roots, stift roots and lianas are conspicuous structures of the rainforest and signs of its diversity. The branches of the trees are often densely covered with mosses, ferns, orchids and bromeliads. Hardly any light penetrates through this multi-layered canopy to the ground, so only a few plants grow there.

Fauna and Flora

This rich and structured forest provides a privileged habitat for many species of insects, spiders, frogs, reptiles and birds, but larger herbivorous animals are rare.

Luxuriant growth on poor soil

Although this ecosystem is the richest and most complex on the planet, the rainforest soil is still fragile, thin and poor. The soils are poorly suited for agriculture. Over a very long period of time, climatic conditions have profoundly deteriorated the chemistry of the rocks and the vegetation draws its nutrients by reusing its own decomposed elements. The ecosystem is highly sensitive to human intervention and change.

A forest – forests

This intertropical forest is not homogeneous according to its geographical location. There are different types of equatorial forests: deep, flooded forests along rivers, swamps, mountains and mangroves on the coast influenced by salt water.

The savannah

The savannah is a plant formation composed mainly of herbaceous plants more or less scattered with trees or shrubs. The animal and plant diversity of the savannah is much lower than that of the equatorial forest. In Africa, these vast expanses of herbaceous plants provide sufficient food for herds, whereas the much more frugal Brazilian savannah leaves room for very few mammals.animals are rare.

Australia, a unique continent

Australia, the smallest continent on earth, is located in the southern hemisphere. Its landscape is marked by desert and semi-arid regions and dry forests of eucalyptus and acacia.
Thanks to its geographical isolation for more than 200 million years, a particular flora and fauna has developed here. Australia‘s fauna includes a significant number of animals that only exist on this continent. 83% of mammals, 89% of reptiles and 93% of amphibians are endemic. Outstanding representatives are the kangaroos, platypus, frilled lizard and thorny devil. In the south-eastern and south-western parts of the continent, the climate is temperate with hot, dry summers and rainy winters. Here you will find forest and bush landscapes, productive pastures and arable land.
This is where most people live and is home to major cities such as Sydney, Melbourne and Adelaide.

East African Rift Valley

A rift valley is a lowland region that forms where Earth’s tectonic plates move apart or rift. Rift valleys are found both on land and at the bottom of the ocean, where they are created by the process of seafloor spreading. Rift valleys differ from river valleys and glacial valleys in that they are created by tectonic activity and not the process of erosion.
The most well-known rift valley on Earth is probably the so-called "Great Rift Valley System" which stretches from the Middle East in the north to Mozambique in the south. The area is geologically active, and features volcanoes, hot springs, geysers, and frequent earthquakes. All of the rift valleys in the “system” are connected, but not part of a single unit.

The Western Rift includes many of the African Great Lakes. The Western Rift is one of the most biodiverse regions in Africa, featuring a narrow corridor of highland forests, snow-capped mountains, savannas, and chains of lakes and wetlands.
The fissure in Kenya's Great Rift Valley, which opened after strong earth movements, is around 15 meters deep and three kilometers long. And the fissure is only the beginning: Researchers believe that a new continent is splitting off in the Horn of Africa, which in the distant future will include the current countries of Somalia, Kenya, Tanzania and half of Ethiopia.

Albertine Rift

Western part of the East African Rift Valley

The Albertine Rift is the western branch of the East African Rift, covering parts of Uganda, the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), Rwanda, Burundi and Tanzania. It extends from the northern end of Lake Albert to the southern end of Lake Tanganyika. The geographical term includes the valley and the surrounding mountains.

Volcanoes

The Virunga massif refers to an area containing a chain of 8 volcanoes that span across the three countries of Rwanda, Uganda D R Congo and covers 3 national parks of Parc National des Virunga (PNV) in the DR Congo, Volcanoes National Park (VNP) in Rwanda and the Mgahinga Gorilla National Park (MGNP) in Uganda. The massif covers an area of over 8000 sqkm and derives its name from the Kinyarwanda word ‘ibirunga’ which literally means the volcanoes. The volcanoes in this chain include Nyiragongo (3,470 meters) and Nyamuragira (3,058 meters) both in the Democratic Republic of Congo, which are the only active ones.
Mt. Karisimbi is the highest of all the 8 volcanoes at 4,507 meters while Mt. Sabyinyo (3,674 meters) is the oldest of the Virunga volcanoes and forms the border of Uganda, Rwanda and D R Congo. Other volcanoes include Mikeno, Muhabura, Bisoke, and Gahinga.
This Volcano chain is a branch of the Albertine Rift Mountains that border the western arm of the East African rift valley.

Rwanda profile

Landscapes are subject to constant change over the millennia. Climatic changes in particular, but also tectonic events such as the sinking of earth plates or the eruption of volcanoes can completely change vegetation forms. In the last centuries of the Holocene, however, humans have ushered in a new age: human intervention in nature and its exploitation, e.g. by burning fossil fuels, cutting down forests and interfering with natural river systems, as well as mere mass reproduction, are increasingly showing the negative effects on the natural foundations of their existence. This period of time up to the present day is now new referred to as the Anthropocene.
These changes can also be seen very clearly in Rwanda. As in the other regions of Africa, the Great Lakes region also experienced strong climatic changes during the Late Holocene (4000 B.C. to the present day), which caused the landscapes to change considerably. Periods of drought alternated with heavy rainy seasons. By 2,500 BC, Rwanda's forests had almost disappeared - but a thousand years later, thanks to a long wet period, they had recovered. The natural vegetation of Rwanda was largely destroyed or changed by humans. Only in island-like areas of the central highlands and in the three nature reserves (Nyungwe, Virunga and the Akagera National Park) does the country's original flora still exist.
The advent of metal processing, the increase in population, the reclamation of swamp valleys into agricultural fields and the increase in livestock farming have changed the forests in the last 2000 years, first in the flatter parts of the country in favor of a savannah and grassland landscape, and later a severe decimation of the original higher altitude forest areas also began. The large forest area stretching from north to south along the rift valley to Lake Kivu has almost disappeared: Remains of it today are the Nyungwe, the Cyamudongo, the Mukura and the Gishwati forest.
Thus, in the first pictures of Rwanda at the end of the 19th century, one sees a very barren, almost exclusively grassy landscape. In the last 150 years, the landscape in Rwanda has developed rapidly and in very contrasting ways: On the one hand, agriculture has spread over the hills - 85% of Rwanda's land is now cultivated, the slopes were needed as arable land to feed the rapidly increasing population. This increased erosion, so that in some parts of the country, such as Ruhango district, the rock became visible. At the same time, however, reforestation measures were taken, swamp valleys were cleared of development to protect the water.
However, construction and sealing of the area continues to increase steadily, human intervention in water systems, the increase in pollution due to the lack of sewage treatment plants are increasingly burdening the quality of the lakes and rivers. The hilly landscape continues to be exposed to major erosion - agriculture, mineral extraction, road construction and urbanization.
The newly emerging global climate change is changing the climate in Rwanda too: rains are becoming heavier but shorter in duration, dry seasons are getting warmer and longer.

History of Rwanda Hydrology

The history of the drainage system of Rwanda represents that from the miocene period (23.03 to 5.3 million years ago) to the Pliocene period (5.3 to 2.6 million years ago) befor the formation of the rift valley the drainage system flowed towards the northern part of the country. In the middle Pliocene, the formation of central African Rift resulted in the creation of a river system in the area, that resembles the modern picture in structure, but not the direction of drainage. In the Pleistocene (2,6 million years ago), a massive breakdown of the rift shoulder between Bugesera and Kibuye takes place in this phase the Masangano area probably retains its northern drainage, while rivers in the south and in cnetral Rwanda drain into the rift via Kibuye, forming lake Kivu. The rift should at Kibuye rise again by 700-1150m and Rwanda again is drained to the north. During upper Pleistocene, the volcanoes in the north of Rwanda rise further, interrupting the drainage of Lake Kivu to the North inside the rift. In Rwanda, the direction of flow of many rivers reverses, the modern Mukungwa is created and the modern Nyabarongo flows from Masangano to the East, flooding all of the Bugesera. Finally, the Akagera drain into Lake Victoria comes into the existence, which leads to the modern-day state.

Energy

Energy and Climate

The fundamental climate driver is the sun.
The energy it radiates drives air currents and causes water to evaporate. This results in the distribution of wind, clouds and precipitation. However, it is not only atmospheric processes or the oceans that play a role in climate, but also the parameters of the Earth's orbit. These change over longer periods of time and thus cause the alternation between ice ages and warm periods. In addition, there are other climate factors such as the distribution of land and sea, the chemical composition of the Earth's atmosphere, the latitude of an area, its altitude and position in relation to the sea, volcanic activity (rarely also meteorite impacts) and vegetation.
In addition, so-called secondary factors also determine the earth's climate. These include the general atmospheric circulation with the major wind systems, ocean currents and the water cycle, as well as regional weather phenomena such as El Niño and La Niña. These climate factors determine which climate zone a country or region belongs to. Defined by temperature, there are five climate zones in each hemisphere: the polar zone, the subpolar zone, the temperate zone, the subtropics and the tropics. However, the factors are also decisive for regional climates. In this respect, the researchers distinguish between high-altitude, urban, maritime and continental climates.
The use of energy resources on Earth play an important role in climate change.

What is Energy

Energy is something like a force that can do something.
Expressed vividly, energy is the ability to do work, give off heat or radiate light. It is therefore necessary if something is to be set in motion, accelerated, lifted, heated or illuminated. Energy is vital because it is needed for all natural processes.

Energy can be contained in a system in different ways. These possibilities are called forms of energy. Examples of forms of energy are kinetic energy, chemical energy, electrical energy or potential energy. Different forms of energy can be converted into each other, whereby the sum of the amounts of energy over the different forms of energy is always the same before and after the energy conversion.

This means: energy is not lost - it is only transformed - but it remains.

Types of Energy:

There are two types of energy, namely:

  • Potential energy:is the stored energy or energy of an object in its position. This energy depends essentially on the composition and position of an object.
  • Kinetic energy:this is obtained when an object accelerates.

Forms of energy:

Potential and Kinetic energy exist in various forms: chemical, thermal, electromagnetic, radiations, gravitational, electric, nuclear energy, etc.
The transformation or conversion from one form to another is at the baseline for the various energy sources that allow us to perform all mankind activities.

The Wimshurst machine is an electrostatic generator that was invented in the late 19th century by British engineer James Wimshurst. It consists of two counter-rotating disks with metal plates attached to their surfaces, which are separated by a small gap. The disks are rotated by a crank, which causes the plates to become charged with static electricity.

Source of energy

The Earth receives from the Sun an uninterrupted flow of energy that, as well as supplying all vital processes (both vegetal and animal), melts the ice and supplies the water cycle between the sea and the atmosphere. From those subsequent transformations of solar energy into various manifestations and stores considered as sources of usable energy for human.

Energy sources are divided in two groups: Renewable energy and Non-renewable energy

Non-renewable energy is energy from fossil fuels (coal, crude oil, natural gas) and uranium, which, once used up, no longer grows back and is therefore no longer available.

Renewable energy is energy extracted from the natural flux and it is provided directly or indirectly by the sun, wind, hydro, biomass and geothermal whose lifetime is beyond our existential needs and challenges.

Rwanda energy primary use is dominated by biomass, which accounts for 86.3% of the total. Rwanda has one of the lowest electricity consumption per capita in the region, and generation capacity is low. 83.3% of Rwandan households use wood for their cooking fuel, followed by charcoal (15.2 %), crop waste (0.8%), gas or biogas (0.2%) and other 6%.
At national level, the use of electricity for lighting changed from 11% in 2010/11 to 20% in 2013/14. Of the 14% of non-biomass primary energy, petroleum products account for 11% (used mainly in the transport sector) and electricity for approximately 3%.

Fossil energy

Fossil energy is obtained from fuels that were formed in geological prehistory from decomposition products of dead plants and animals. These include lignite, hard coal, peat, natural gas and petroleum.

Fossil fuels are based on the carbon cycle and thus enable stored (solar) energy of past times to be utilised today. The technical exploitation of fossil fuels, initially almost exclusively coal, has enabled continuous economic growth since the mid-19th century in Europe and North America, and later in Asia. In 2005, 81 % of the world's energy needs were met from fossil sources. The energy content of the fossil fuels listed is based on the carbon content.

When burned with oxygen, energy is released in the form of heat and oxides, always including carbon dioxide. Therefore, the combustion of fossil fuels is highly polluting and harmful to the climate, both locally and globally

Petroleum

Crude oil is a lipophilic mixture of substances mainly consisting of hydrocarbons (including methane) that is embedded in the earth's crust. With more than 17,000 components, crude petroleum (crude oil) is one of the most complex mixtures of organic substances that occur naturally on earth. Crude oil is formed by the conversion of organic substances such as plants and animals (plankton) through pressure and heat.

Separation and conversion processes transform crude oil into a variety of intermediate products that serve as the basis for the production of fuels and as raw materials for industry. The latter include above all starting materials for numerous products of the chemical industry, such as plastics, varnishes, paints and medicines. Petroleum is called "black gold" (because of its enormous economic importance).

Vast, underexplored, and now known to be full of petroleum resources, the East African Rift System is one of the most exciting areas for onshore oil and gas investment in the world. These region has a great potential, with 6 billion barrels of oil discovered in Lake Albert in Uganda and 600 million barrels of oil discovered on Kenya’s side of Lake Turkana. Exploration is ongoing in Lake Tanganyika and Lake Malawi. Rwanda is considering partnering with oil and gas companies for the next stage of exploring Lake Kivu for more hydrocarbons – beside extracting Methane gas.

Peat

Peat is an organic sediment that is formed in bogs. In its dried state, peat is combustible. It forms from the accumulation of undecomposed or incompletely decomposed plant matter and represents the first stage of charring.

According to a peat master plan developed in 2012, the peat-to-power potential is estimated at around 230 MW of peat generation based on estimated reserves of 12.5 million tons (JICA, 2016). About 77% of peat reserves are near Akanyaru, Nyabarongo rivers and the Rwabusoro Plains.

However, the negative environmental impacts of draining the wetlands are controversial and a costbenefit calculation is rather negative. Peat wetlands are very often special ecological places that have a distinctive composition of fauna and flora species. Various birds, orchids, carnivorous plants are typical of peat bogs and swamps. Peat extraction or general peat disturbance in those habitants sometimes have high negative impact on this ecology. In Rwanda, the Kamiranzovu swamp in Nyungwe National Park is one biggest and most ancient mountain forest swamp in central Africa.

Nuclear energy

Nuclear or atomic energy is produced by nuclear fission in a process that involves bombarding a uranium atom with neutrons to split it in two. This releases large amounts of heat, which is then used to generate electricity.

Accidents have serious consequences:
The safety systems of nuclear power plants are very high. However, the human element always has some influence.

Disposal of nuclear waste:A major disadvantage is the disposal of nuclear waste. Nuclear waste takes many, many years to lose its radioactivity and danger.

Limited resources:
Unlike other energy options, nuclear energy is not renewable as uranium has to be mined and is not regenerated.

External dependence:
There is dependence on the outside. Only a few countries have uranium mines. Furthermore, not all countries have nuclear technology.

Advantage

1- High energy density:
Uranium is the element commonly used in nuclear power plants to generate electricity. This has the ability to store enormous amounts of energy: Just one gram of uranium is equivalent to 18 litres of petrol and one kilo produces about the same energy as 100 tonnes of coal (Castells, 2012).

2- Cheaper than fossil fuels
Basically, the cost of uranium appears to be much more expensive than oil or petrol, but if we take into account that only small amounts of this element are required to produce significant amounts of energy, eventually the cost becomes even less than that of fossil fuels.

3- Availability
A nuclear power plant has the quality to work all the time around the clock 365 days a year to supply a city with electricity. This is due to the fact that the refuelling period is annual or 6 months, depending on the plant. Other energy rely on a steady supply of fuel (like coal plants) or are intermittent and are limited by climate (like renewable sources).

4- It emits less greenhouse gases (GHG) than fossil fuels.
The process in the nuclear power plant does not emit GHGs because it does not require fossil fuels. However, the emissions that do occur occur throughout the life cycle of the plant; construction, operation, extraction and milling of uranium and dismantling of the nuclear power plant. (Sovacool, 2008).

5- Produces little waste
The waste produced by a nuclear power plant is extremely hazardous and harmful to the environment. However, the amount is relatively small compared to other activities and adequate safety measures are applied that can remain isolated from the environment without posing a risk.

Disadvantage

1 - Uranium is a non-renewable resource.
Historical data from many countries show that on average no more than 50-70% of uranium can be mined. Uranium concentrations of less than 0.01% are no longer profitable because a larger amount of rock must be mined and the energy used is greater than what could be produced with biomass. (Dittmar, 2013).

However, it should be noted that the longevity of radioactive elements depends on the particular atom: iodide-131, for example, is very shortlived and has a half-life of 8.02 days. This means that its radiation intensity is halved after 8.02 days. The situation is different for caesium-137, which is much more dangerous for the human organism, because this radionuclide has a half-life of 30.17 years. Nuclear power alone is not an alternative to oil, gas and coal fuels, as 10 thousand nuclear power plants are needed to replace the world's fossil fuel energy needs. In fact, there are only 486 in the world.

4- Dependent on fossil fuels
Countries concerned with nuclear energy are dependent on fossil fuels. The nuclear fuel cycle includes not only the process of generating electricity at the plant, but also encompasses a range of activities from the exploration and use of uranium mines to the decommissioning and shutdown of the nuclear power plant.

Methan gas

Lake Kivu, with a depth of up to 450 metres, stores huge amounts of methan gas.

In the depths of Lake Kivu, different parts of the earth drift apart. As a result, gases such as methane (CH4 ) and carbon dioxide (CO₂ ) escape and accumulate in the lake water, just like in active volcanoes. CO₂ is particularly dangerous, as it is odorless and deadly in large concentrations. The methane gas is kept deep in the lake by the water pressure and used by a pumping station to generate energy.

Lake Kivu methane gas is extracted and used for the first time in Rwanda by Union chimique de Belge with a gas pilot plant at cape Rubona in 1963 to supply the Bralirwa brewery.

The US-based company Contour Global has anchored in 2018 a 50 by 30 meter production platform in the middle of the lake, which currently produces around 25 MW of energy.

In the process, the water is sucked out of the depths and the gases are extracted. Gas and water separate automatically as soon as the pressure drops on the way to the surface - methane separates at a depth of 120 meters, carbon dioxide at 15 meters. The system directs CO₂ back into the water and methane through a pipe to the shore. There it is burned, the hot air drives a turbine to produce electricity.

Contour Global plans to increase capacity to 100 MW in the future with three more platforms. Lake Kivu holds enough methane gas to provide 700 megawatts for 55 years and could supply all of Rwanda and neighbouring countries with electricity.

Biomass

When we talk about "energy from biomass" or "bioenergy", we are referring to energy production from plants or plant residues.

Rwanda energy primary use is dominated by biomass, which accounts for 86.3% of the total.

There are different types of raw materials that can be used bioenergetically:

  • agricultural crops cultivated specifically for this purpose, such as maize or rapeseed
  • Fast-growing woody plants
  • Waste and residual materials from agriculture, households and industry (e.g. wood chips from the wood industry, used fat from food production, but also sewage sludge)

How can we use biomass?

The types of use are correspondingly diverse:

  • as biogas (mostly methane) to generate electricity and heat
  • in liquid form as fuel from vegetable oil (biodiesel)
  • solid as pellets or logs, also for heat and power generation.

70% of Rwandan households use wood for their cooking fuel, followed by charcoal (20%), crop waste (0.8%), gas or biogas (0.2%) and other 9%. (in 2018)

Advantages of biomass:
The use of biomass can thus have many advantages: It offers an alternative to fossil fuels and, when burned, releases only as much climatedamaging carbon dioxide (CO₂ ) as was bound in the plant during its growth.

Disadvantages of biomass:
Biomass is not available in unlimited quantities - partly because the land available for growing plants for energy use is not available for growing food. In the long term, population growth, rising meat consumption, climate change and also bioenergy will very likely lead to price increases for agricultural commodities.

Biomass is considered by far the most inefficient form of renewable energy use, as its efficiency - the amount of energy produced on a given area - is only 0.5 to 1 percent. By comparison, the efficiency of solar modules is about 20 per cent.

Biogas

Biogas is obtained naturally through the decomposition of organic waste or renewable raw materials and is therefore not a fossil fuel. In a fermentation process, organic substances are microbiologically degraded in a warm, humid environment and in the absence of air.

In a biogas plant, a controlled, biological decomposition process (digestion or fermentation) takes place, which converts the organic biomass mainly into water, carbon dioxide and methane. The methane contained in the water-saturated biogas is about 60 % valuable. The higher the proportion, the more energy-rich the gas.

However, methane is a powerful greenhouse gas. If methane, which has a 25 to 30 times stronger heating effect on the climate than CO₂ , escapes into the atmosphere during the operation of a biogas plant, this has negative effects on the environment.

Government has put in place an elaborate program for disseminating bio digesters in households, schools and prisons to reduce demand for firewood and charcoal and reducing household pollution since 2007 (the National Domestic Biogas Program /NDBP). The government subsidized biogas technologies with a 50% subsidy and local microfinances provided assistance in the program. The One cow by the family is also a program which supported the biogas project in Rwanda.

Solar energy

Solar energy is the energy of solar radiation.

In terms of quantity, the largest use of solar energy is to heat the earth so that biological existence in the known forms is possible in the near-surface region, followed by photosynthesis of algae and higher plants.

Most organisms, including humans, depend on solar energy either directly (as herbivores) or indirectly (as carnivores). Fuel and building materials also come from it. Solar energy is also responsible for creating air pressure differences in the atmosphere that lead to wind. The earth's water cycle is also driven by solar energy.

The geographical location of Rwanda endows it with sufficient solar radiation intensity varying between 4.3 and 5.2 kWh /m2/day and peak sun hours of approximately 5 hours per day. The government has developed measures to encourage the use of solar energy in different sectors such as in some administrative offices, schools and hotels.

Wind energy

Wind energy is one of the most environmentally friendly, cleanest and safest energy resources. It is considered one of the most promising renewable energy sources due to its worldwide availability, low cost and level of technological development. Meanwhile, it plays a significant role in the expansion of renewable energy. Internationally, wind power plants can now produce energy more cheaply than fossil fuel power plants. Wind energy represents the most economical expansion potential among renewable energy sources. Wind turbines can be used to generate electricity in all climatic zones, at sea and in all land locations (coastal, inland, mountainous). Being located close to the equator, Rwanda’s inherent resource potential for wind energy is low. Nevertheless, there are some sites with high potentiality that have been identified based on preliminary satellite data: Nyamagabe, Ngoma, Kayonza, Rulindo and Nyabihu.

Hydropower

In a hydropower plant, water is dammed at a high level in order to drive a turbine with the falling water, which converts this energy into electricity The advantages of hydropower plants are manifold: By using water as a renewable energy source, fossil fuels are conserved, which contributes to climate protection. Furthermore, hydropower plants are independent of weather and time and can therefore not only produce electricity 24 hours a day, but also store it when needed.

The main disadvantages of hydropower plants are their negative impact on the ecology: the construction of hydropower plants alters natural rivers and disturbs the groundwater balance; it interferes with the habitats of fish and aquatic plants.

Hydropower plants include both domestic and regional hydropower plants. Domestic hydropower consists of small and medium-size hydropower located in specific sites and Ntaruka hydropower is the first to be constructed in Rwanda in 1957. Due to Rwanda topography and hydrology, there are about 333 potential sites for domestic hydropower countrywide. Regional hydropower refers to joint hydro resources such as Rusizi on the border with DRC and Rusumo Falls on the border with Tanzania.

The Nyabarongo River hydropower dam, reservoir, surge shaft, tunnel and power house have been built by using the most modern technology to add an additional 30% to Rwanda’s installed generation capacity in 2014

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy refers to thermal energy (heat) stored within the earth. It is therefore also known as geothermal energy. The centre of the Earth has a similar temperature to the surface of the Sun (circa 5000-6000 °C). The planet cools down towards the surface. Hot springs and geothermal power plants reach depths where there is a high temperature difference from the Earth's surface and make the heat of the Earth's interior usable for humans. Near-surface geothermal energy refers to the use of geothermal energy up to a depth of approx. 400 m, which can be used directly.

Deep geothermal energy is the use of deposits that are tapped at depths greater than 400 m below ground level and can be used as electricity after conversion by a geothermal power plant. A geothermal power plant does not emit carbon dioxide during electricity production. For this reason alone, this type of energy generation is environmentally friendly.

The geothermal processes in the earth's interior run permanently, so electricity generation can be described as regenerative. However, the boreholes can be problematic, as they reach over kilometres into the depths and can trigger earth tremors such as faults.

Rwanda has geothermal potential, which is manifested in the form of hot springs. Some prospect areas have been identified for potential exploration: Kinigi, Kalisimbi, Rubavu and Bugarama.

However, the impacts on the environment in the Virunga region have not yet been properly researched - for example, on the population of gorillas.

Hydrogen

Hydrogen is a chemical element and, with a share of around 75 percent, a large part of the mass of our solar system consists of this gas. It is part of almost every organic compound on earth and is traded by many experts as the raw material of the future. As an energy storage device, it can be obtained from renewable energies, for example, and used in almost all energy-consuming areas - i.e. mobility, heat and electricity.

Compared to fossil fuels, but also to some renewable energies, hydrogen offers many advantages. With the help of hydrogen, excess electrical energy from solar or wind power plants can be stored well for times when the sun is not shining and the wind is not blowing. Tied to gas, electricity from renewable energies is also transportable in this way and can be used anywhere with a time delay.

Another advantage of hydrogen is that when it "burns", that is, when it reacts with oxygen, predominantly water is produced. This results in fewer CO₂ emissions and fewer fossil fuels being used Another possibility for producing hydrogen is electrolysis with the help of electricity. For example, electricity from solar or wind power plants is used to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. In this way, the electricity can be stored effectively and for a long time Alternatives to the reforming process and electrolysis are fermentation of biomass, e.g. extraction from green algae. An example of this is Lake Victoria, which is suffering from a massive glut of algae. The gasification of biomass, i.e. the addition of oxygen, is the most cost-effective way of producing hydrogen to date

Greenhouse gas

Global warming

Greenhouse gases are those gases in the atmosphere that influence the energy balance of the earth. They thus cause the so-called greenhouse effect.

What is a greenhouse effect?
Explained schematically, the earth's surface is heated by solar radiation. The earth's surface reflects the incident radiation as long-wave thermal radiation, which is partially absorbed by the atmospheric greenhouse gases. By subsequently releasing the absorbed energy, part of the radiation is radiated back to the earth and the earth's surface and lower atmospheric layer are warmed. This overall process is called the greenhouse effect.

The human-made greenhouse effect
However, the natural balance between radiation in and radiation out is disturbed by man-made greenhouse gases. Since the beginning of industrialization, emissions of climate-affecting gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane and nitrous oxide have risen dramatically Today, 40% more CO₂ is measured than at the beginning of the industrial age. As a result, the earth's surface has warmed globally by 0.85°C since the beginning of the 20th century

However, the combustion of fossil fuels directly releases large amounts of carbon that have been bound in fossil fuels for millions of years as carbon dioxide (CO₂) into the atmosphere, thus increasing the natural greenhouse effect of the atmosphere.

Effect of the greenhouse effect:
The current weather system in the different parts of the world may change massively. An increase in precipitation is expected in the humid tropics.

2. higher wind speeds, more storms and heavy precipitation.
Higher temperatures cause more water to evaporate. The condensation energy released in the troposphere causes an increase in the air pressure gradient and thus in the wind currents.

3. increase in weather extremes
The variability of weather will increase, with heavy precipitation and periods of drought, heat and cold periods increasing in duration, extent and frequency. Due to the accumulation of extreme weather events, tropical regions will experience increased heavy rainfall and associated massive flooding.

4. existential threat to forests
A temperature increase of several °C within a century would, after the large-scale destruction of forests in the tropics (for land reclamation), also threaten forests in temperate and northern latitudes. The trees, which are already endangered by increased UV and pollutant exposure, would not survive the climate stress and the emerging strong winds.

5. increase in migration
People would have to leave ancestral settlement areas, as they can no longer be cultivated. Loss of arable land.

Ocean pollution

By 2050, the total amount of plastic waste will exceed the total weight of all living creatures in the Earth's oceans.

Marine pollution is a global problem. Ocean currents swirl rubbish into giant rubbish patches – the largest of which floats halfway between Hawaii and California and is twice the size of the US state of Texas, according to a 2018 study by the Ocean Cleanup Foundation.

In the 1950s, just under 1.5 million tonnes of plastic were produced per year; today it is almost 400 million tonnes. And far too much of it ends up in the sea.
About 75 per cent of all marine litter consists of plastics. According to the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), there are now up to 18,000 pieces of plastic of various sizes floating on every square kilometre of ocean surface. Every year, about 8 million tonnes of plastic end up in the sea. Plastic does not dissolve on its own, but is mechanically broken down by wave action and crushed into microplastic under the stress of UV radiation. This slowly sinks to the seabed. Most of the plastic (up to 99 per cent) is below the surface of the sea.

This makes it impossible to estimate the actual amount of waste.

Most plastic enters the sea and ocean via Asia's longest rivers, the Yangtze and Yellow Rivers in China and Pakistan's second largest river, the Indus.

The ocean has become one of the dirtiest places in the world. Plastic bags, plastic bottles, straws and toothbrushes are floating in five large rubbish patches. If we do nothing about it, by 2050 there will be more plastic swimming in the sea than fish.

Mitigation against environmental degradation

Waste management

All materials drawn out of the environment end up as waste either directly (from the production sector) or indirectly (from the recycling sector). The modern principles of waste management are based on the assumption that all consumption waste flows back to the recycling sector either for recycling and/or for disposal.

The following are main principals of waste management:

  • A Polluter pays: The party responsible for producing waste (pollution) pays for the damage done to the environment.
  • B Resource Efficiency:Using the Earth's limited resources in a sustainable manner while minimizing environmental impact.
  • C Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR):A policy approach under which producers are given a significant responsibility – financial and/or physical – for the treatment or disposal of post-consumer products (waste)

Life cycle of a product: The product life cycle illustrates a process in which raw materials are turned into products and consumed. Once their useful life expires, they are discarded as waste. Thereafter waste can be reused, recycled or disposed of.

The waste management hierarchy makes preventing waste a top priority. However, when waste is produced, priority is given to preparing it for re-use, then recycling and then recovery. The least desirable option is waste disposal.

Waste disposal options

A - Open dumping:Still in practice in many countries, dumping of waste in open spaces is the worst disposal option. It consumes lots of lands, pollutes the air with greenhouse gases and contaminates the groundwater.

B - Waste to energy:The process turns the nonrecyclable waste into secure energy. It is an alternative to landfills as the trash is used to provide electricity, heat or biogas.

C - Sanitary landfills:waste is buried either underground or in large piles and sealed in with impermeable synthetic botton liners to prevent groundwater pollution. The landfill is organized in compacted layers, the layers alternate between waste and soil. This alternation of materials reduces odors and allows for more rapid decomposition, which is the breakdown of materials. When a landfill is full, it is sealed and covered in a thick layer of soil or clay. Once the landfill has been evaluated and considered safe, it can be converted into a park or open space for human use.

Green Policy in Rwanda

The climate variations observed in Rwanda come to add to the major socio-ecological disruptions taking place, such as deforestation, desertification, overuse of land and natural resources. (2) Enhancing functional natural ecosystems and managing biosafety,

According to this law, manufacturers or retailers, for example, are required to put mechanisms in place to collect and segregate used plastics for recycling.

Geology

Rwanda is located in the East African Rift System, a region characterized by volcanic and tectonic activity. The country has a complex geology, with rocks ranging in age from Precambrian to Quaternary. The geology of Rwanda is dominated by Precambrian basement rocks, which consist of granites, gneisses, and schists. In the western part of the country, there are volcanic rocks, including basaltic lavas, tuffs, and agglomerates. The region also has several mineral deposits, including tin, tungsten, tantalum, and gold, which are associated with the granitic rocks. Additionally, there are hot springs and geothermal areas in the western part of the country, indicating the presence of geothermal resources that can be harnessed for energy.

Igneous Rocks: Formed from the cooling and solidification of molten magma. Classified into intrusive (cooled below the surface, e.g., granite) and extrusive (cooled at the surface, e.g., basalt) types. They provide insights into Earth's internal processes.

Sedimentary Rocks: Created by the accumulation, compression, and cementation of sediments (fragments of older rocks, minerals, and organic material). They preserve clues about Earth's past environments, fossils, and changing landscapes. Examples include limestone (formed from marine shells), sandstone, and shale.

Metamorphic Rocks: Arise from the alteration of existing rocks due to heat and pressure, often deep within Earth's crust. Original minerals recrystallize into new forms, creating a range of textures. Examples include marble (from limestone) and slate (from shale). Metamorphism reveals the tectonic forces shaping the planet.

Stalagmite and Stalactite

A stalagmite is a type of mineral deposit that forms on the floor of a cave or cavern through the process of mineral precipitation. Stalagmites grow upwards from the cave floor and are often associated with stalactites, which hang down from the cave ceiling. Both stalagmites and stalactites are examples of speleothems, which are mineral formations that develop in caves over long periods of time.

Minerology

Gemstone

Evolution

Animal evolution

Animal evolution refers to the process by which living organisms that belong to the animal kingdom have changed over time through successive generations. Evolutionary changes can result from various mechanisms, such as genetic mutations, natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and other factors.

There have been several different periods of evolution throughout the history of life on Earth. Here are some of the major periods of evolution:

  • Pre-Cambrian Era (4.6 billion years ago to 541 million years ago): This period covers most of the Earth's history, and it saw the emergence of the first prokaryotic organisms, followed by the evolution of eukaryotic cells and multicellular life.
  • Paleozoic Era (541 million years ago to 252 million years ago): This era saw the Cambrian explosion, the rapid diversification of animal life, and the emergence of many new body plans and adaptations. The Paleozoic Era also saw the evolution of plants, the colonization of land by vertebrates, and the development of amphibians and reptiles.
  • Mesozoic Era (252 million years ago to 66 million years ago): The Mesozoic Era saw the evolution of dinosaurs, birds, and many other reptilian and mammalian groups. This era also saw the emergence of flowering plants, which became dominant on land.
  • Cenozoic Era (66 million years ago to present): This era saw the diversification of mammals, the emergence of primates, and the evolution of humans. The Cenozoic Era also saw the evolution of birds and the continued diversification of flowering plants.
  • Paleogene Period (66 million years ago to 23 million years ago): This period saw the recovery and diversification of life after the mass extinction event that wiped out the dinosaurs. Mammals, including primates, underwent significant evolutionary diversification during this period.
  • Neogene Period (23 million years ago to 2.6 million years ago): The Neogene period saw the emergence of many modern groups of mammals, including apes, elephants, and cats. The climate also underwent significant cooling, leading to the evolution of grasslands and the diversification of grazing animals.
  • Quaternary Period (2.6 million years ago to present): The Quaternary period saw the emergence and diversification of hominins, including the evolution of modern humans. It also saw the evolution and extinction of many large mammals, such as mammoths and saber-toothed cats, and the emergence of modern ecosystems.

Overall, the history of life on Earth has been characterized by a series of evolutionary changes that have resulted in an enormous diversity of organisms and ecological niches.

Human evolution

Human evolution refers to the evolutionary process by which modern humans (Homo sapiens) and their ancestral hominid species have emerged over time. The evolution of humans is a complex and ongoing process that has taken place over millions of years, and it involves various hominid species and their adaptations to changing environmental conditions. Here are some of the major stages of human evolution:

  • Australopithecus: This genus includes several species of bipedal hominids that lived between 4.2 and 2.9 million years ago. They had smaller brains than modern humans but were the first hominids to walk upright on two legs.
  • Homo habilis: This species lived about 2.8 million years ago and is believed to have been the first hominid to use stone tools.
  • Homo erectus: This species lived between 2 million and 100,000 years ago and had a larger brain than earlier hominids. They were the first hominids to leave Africa and colonize other parts of the world.
  • Homo neanderthalensis: This species lived between 400,000 and 40,000 years ago and had a similar brain size to modern humans. They were adapted to living in cold environments and had a robust build.
  • Homo sapiens: This is the species to which modern humans belong. They emerged in Africa about 300,000 years ago and spread to other parts of the world, gradually replacing other hominid species. They are characterized by their larger brain size, advanced culture, and complex social organization.

Overall, the evolution of humans is a complex and ongoing process that involves the development of various adaptations, including the use of tools, the evolution of larger brains, and the emergence of advanced social behaviors. The study of human evolution helps us understand our origins and how we have become the species that we are today.
